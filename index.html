Regional-Scale

Hydrology & Water Quality Modeling


Originally developed in the early 1990s (Wigmosta et al., 1994), the Distributed Hydrology-Vegetation Model (DHSVM) is a spatially distributed land surface hydrologic model that explicitly represents the effects of topography and vegetation on water fluxes. DHSVM is typically applied at high spatial resolutions (30~150 m) for watersheds of up to 104 km2 at sub-daily timescales. It has been widely applied to model water fluxes in predominantly mountainous and partially urbanized watershed.

Since I joined the Land Surface Hydrology Group in University of Washington in August 2012, I have been further developing DHSVM to extend its capabilities to assess water quality response to changes in climate and land use at regional scales. This new modeling system, DHSVM-WQ, fully integrates sediment transport modules, urban water quality modules, nutrient cycling modules, and stream temperature modules into the framework of DHSVM, and allows simulating at sub-daily timesteps sediment transport, nutrients (N and P) fate and transport, urban storm water contaminants and stream temperature in urban, agricultural and natural systems.

 DHSVM-WQ was tested in four watersheds in the Puget Sound basin characterized by a varying degree of urbanization (20%~90%). A manuscript based on this work is under preparation for submittal to a peer-reviewed journal (Sun et al., in prep).

DHSVM-WQ will be further applied to 15 major partially-urbanized watersheds tributary to Puget Sound and to 14 major watersheds tributary to the Connecticut River to study the impact of climate and land use changes on regional urban quality.

Publications:

Sun, N., J. Yearsley, B. Nijssen, and D.P. Lettenmaier (in prep): Simulating the Response of Urban Water Quality to Climate and Land Use Change in Partially Urbanized Basins. Hydrol. Process.

Stream Temperature Modeling


RBM (Yearsley, 2009) is a one-dimensional stream temperature model that simulates water temperature in a vector-based stream network. The computational framework is based on an Eulerian-Lagrangian particle tracking scheme, which is highly accurate in advection-dominated hydraulic regimes and is highly scalable in space and time. The model was initially developed for estimating the Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) for water temperature in the Columbia River system.

I modified RBM and DHSVM in such a manner that RBM can make direct use of grid-based flow and thermal energy output from DHSVM to simulate water temperature in a vector-based stream network. In addition, I developed a new algorithm within DHSVM to take into account the riparian vegetation shading effect on downward solar radiation.

This newly developed DHSVM-RBM (check DHSVM-RBM website) provides dynamic predictions of stream temperature at a high spatial and temporal resolution for a broad range of spatial scales from local to regional river systems. DHSVM-RBM was applied to a small urban basin, Mercer Creek in Bellevue, Washington to simulate stream temperature response to urbanization and riparian vegetation alteration (Sun et al., in press). Using the same modeling system, we have also estimated thermal loading changes in response to climate and land use changes for the 15 major basins tributary to Puget Sound. A publication based on this work is under preparation for submittal to a peer-reviewed journal (Cao et al., in prep).

Publications:
