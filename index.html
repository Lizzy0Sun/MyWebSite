Regional-Scale

Hydrological & Water Quality Modeling


Originally developed in the early 1990s (Wigmosta et al., 1994), the Distributed Hydrology-Vegetation Model (DHSVM) is a spatially distributed land surface hydrologic model that explicitly represents the effects of topography and vegetation on water fluxes. DHSVM is typically applied at high spatial resolutions (30~150 m) for watersheds of up to 104 km2 at sub-daily timescales. It has been widely applied to model water fluxes in predominantly mountainous and partially urbanized watershed.

Since I joined the Land Surface Hydrology Group at the University of Washington in August 2012, I have focused on simulating water quality reponse to climate change and urbanization on the regional scale. To acheive the research goals, I developed DHSVM-WQ (DHSVM-WATER QUALITY), which fully integrates sediment transport modules, urban water quality modules, nutrient cycling modules, and stream temperature modules into the DHSVM framework, and allows simulating at sub-daily timesteps sediment transport, nutrients (N and P) fate and transport, urban storm water contaminants and stream temperature in urban and natural systems.

DHSVM-WQ was tested in four urban catchments located in the Puget Sound basin characterized by a varying degree of urbanization (20%~90%). A manuscript based on this work is in preparation for submittal to a peer-reviewed journal (Sun et al., in prep).

DHSVM-WQ will be further applied to 15 major partially-urbanized watersheds tributary to Puget Sound and to 14 major watersheds tributary to the Connecticut River to study the impact of climate and land use changes on regional water quality.

Publications:

Sun, N., J. Yearsley, B. Nijssen, and D.P. Lettenmaier (in prep): Simulating the Response of Urban Water Quality to Climate and Land Use Change in Partially Urbanized Basins. Hydrol. Process.

Stream Temperature Modeling


RBM (Yearsley, 2009) is a one-dimensional stream temperature model that simulates water temperature in a vector-based stream network. The computational framework is based on an Eulerian-Lagrangian particle tracking scheme, which is highly accurate in advection-dominated hydraulic regimes and is highly scalable in space and time. The model was initially developed for estimating the Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) for water temperature in the Columbia River system.

In order to assess the impact on stream temperature of land use/climate change and riparian strategies, I developed a coupled land hydrology and stream temperature model, DHSVM-RBM, which is applicable at a wide range of spatiotemporal scales. DHSVM-RBM was successfully applied to a small urban basin, Mercer Creek in Bellevue, Washington to simulate the stream temperature response to urbanization and riparian vegetation alteration scenarios (Sun et al., 2014). Using the same modeling system, we projected the impact of climate and land use change on the thermal loading to Puget Sound, leading to a manuscript (in prep) for which I share co-authorship (Cao et al., in prep).

DHSVM-RBM has been made freely available online to download. Meanwhile, the source code, tutorial, update history of DHSVM-RBM have been well documented on the DHSVM-RBM web page

Human Decision Driven Green Infrastructure Modeling


I developed a modeling framework that couples SWMM with multi-GI implementation scenarios driven by citizen preference and government planning, respectively. The likelihood of citizen participation in GI implementation was analyzed based on block-by-block survey outputs, which was then translated into spatial inputs into the model for projecting stormwater benefits at the catchment scale (Sun and Hall, 2013). The simulation results indicated that both the “government participation” and “household participation” scenarios, compared to the baseline (without GI) condition, would contribute to a modest reduction in stormwater peak flow (>4 %) and total runoff volume (>5 %) across all studided catchments. This study provided decision makers with a scientific methodology to quantify how human decisions can shape ecosystem function, and thus support sustainable stormwater management planning while addressing citizen preferences and needs.

Publications:

Sun, N., M. Hall (2013). Coupling human preferences with biophysical processes: modeling the effect of citizen attitudes on potential urban stormwater runoff.
